## Cantor's Diagonalization Argument

Georg Cantor's diagonalization argument (usually presented as a proof, popularly disseminated using decimal notation and the comparison of the set of natural numbers and a subset of real numbers from 0.0 to 1.0) is flawed (at least in the decimal N to R comparison representation I am familiar with), but to begin, this post will rehash (as bast as formattingly possible) the argument, with follow-up entries discussing the problems identified. The sub-series will conclude with a generalization about infinity that most folks are either not familiar with or are intentionally ignoring to continue the incongruent argument.

What kicked off this disillusionment with drivel mathematics was a video posted to YouTube by popular content creator Veritasium, "Math's Fundabmental Flaw", in which at about 225 seconds in (https://youtu.be/HeQX2HjkcNo?t=225), set theory is introduced, and the argument is discussed. I won't rehash Veritasium's coverage of the argument, but it is where the errors in it were first realized.

Cantor's argument goes like this:

1. List the natural numbers in a table in the left column.
1. Assume we have reached infinity at the end of the left column.
1. List random real numbers in the table in the right column.
1. Derive a new real number and put at the bottom of the right column.
1. Assume this new real number does not exist in the right column.
1. Conclude there are different sizes of infinities.
1. Birth the idea of aleph numbers (N has aleph zero, other sets might different aleph numbers - one, two, etc)

In order to derive a new real number, we need a function to derive a new digit. We'll use the one in Veritasium's video:

    d' = {
      d <= 8 ? d' = d + 1
      d == 9 ? d' = d - 1
    }

We then identify each digit in the right column on a diagonal (hence the name of the argument) starting with the tenth's place 0.(7)438016589139381... to create the new real number:

|N|R [0.0-1.0]|
|-|-|
|1|0.(7)438016589139381...|
|2|0.9(3)81404891941293...|
|3|0.19(9)2840002984918...|
|4|0.248(9)914004598189...|
|5|0.9904(9)18938409198...|
|6|0.88734(9)2843948283...|
|7|0.839184(9)809506045...|
|8|0.4837839(4)83743831...|
|9|0.28472848(4)7277763...|
|10|0.898478772(6)356129...|

Applying the `d'` function, the new real number is 0.8488888557.....

Because this number is between 0.0 and 1.0 while also having the property that each digit is different in at least one position (along the diagonal) for each row, then the new real number between 0.0 and 1.0 doesn't already exist in the right column.

Therefore, because this new number doesn't already exist in the right column, and the natural numbers has reached infinity, the sizes of the set of N and the subset of 0.0 to 1.0 of the real numbers have different sizes of infinity, and therefore the infinity sizes of N and R are different. Because they are different we need aleph numbers to describe the infinitieness of the size of a set.

Now, you may be wondering, well, that's a weird d-prime function (agreed), and maybe you are remembering the old fun school-yard discussions of the biggest number you could count to, and there's that one individual that just keeps +1'ing everyone's biggest number. That is your intuition kicking in, and unlike some proofs, we'll actually integrate it in the subsequent entries to identify the errors, and figure out that the argument is indeed only an argument, and not rigorous enough for a proof.
